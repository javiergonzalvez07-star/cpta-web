We live in an age where information is abundant, but clarity is scarce. Social media rewards short and fast information, which favors attractive but content without a real foundation published in the heat of the moment, rather than long, slow reflections written and published with a colder mind. In many cases, posts are not based on data, but on feelings, and it is becoming increasingly difficult to see a post on Twitter without placing it into a side: yours or the opposite one.

However, this is not just about the internet. This polarization is rooted in our society, from television to political parties, which often encourage this division for their own benefit.

At this point, it is worth asking: what is polarization really, and why does it happen? Polarization is a psychological process that simplifies different opinions into two teams: “Us vs Them.” This is harmful because people end up judging the truth of an idea by its origin, not by its effectiveness. As a result, many people believe they have solid opinions when, in reality, they are simply repeating the views of their own side and criticizing those of the opposite side without evaluating them with a clear mind. When people think in terms of sides rather than effectiveness, critical thinking disappears, because every idea becomes an identity.

This phenomenon is not new. The existence of very different opinions and factions is something natural. After the French Revolution, before Napoleon, different factions emerged and shaped France, such as the Girondins, the Jacobins and, within these, the Mountain. These groups began from the same idea — the revolution — but over time they started to diverge and their positions became more radical. In the period between the French Revolution and Napoleon’s rule, different groups with very different ideas came to power, but there was someone who knew how to navigate all these changes while avoiding polarization and placing the good of his country first: Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand.

Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand was one of the most skilled politicians and diplomats of the French Revolution. He survived and maintained influence under multiple different regimes. Many called him an opportunist, but Talleyrand’s reality was much deeper than that. While most people, both in his time and in ours, built their political ideas around their sense of group identity, Talleyrand did not tie himself to one side. While others unconsciously asked themselves, “Does this fit with my side?”, he consciously asked: “Does this serve the reason of state right now?”

Here we find several key ideas. The first is the “reason of state”. For Talleyrand, the most important thing was not which regime existed in society, but where that regime was leading the country and whether it fulfilled the role that the state should have in society. On the other hand, we also find the words “right now.” This is important because, for Talleyrand, there should be no dogmatism: depending on the current state of the country, different policies should be used as tools to reach an end. What matters is reaching the end through the right tools, not making the end itself the use of your favorite tool. It is like a mechanic using the same screwdriver for every type of screw: it makes no sense.

Many people will look at someone like Talleyrand and call him opportunistic or indecisive, but moderation is not indecision; it is intelligent flexibility. Agreeing with certain aspects of both sides depending on the situation does not make you incoherent, but critical, complex and analytical.

If society can learn something from Talleyrand, it is that ideas should be evaluated by their effectiveness, not by their origin. Politics should not be a dogmatic tribal identity, but an instrument to achieve the wellbeing of society, with many tools of different shapes and sizes available to reach that goal. Ultimately, it is not about being on one side, on the other, or in the middle, but about thinking for oneself what is in the best interest of the situation.
