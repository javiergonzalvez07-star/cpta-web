Ever since science has existed, scientists have dreamt of finding what they called a "theory of everything". What they meant by this was a theory capable of explaining everything in the universe, something which has not been achieved to this day.

In the nineteenth century, a French scientist, Marquis de Laplace, began to argue that the universe was completely deterministic, which meant there could be certain rules that apply to it as a whole. This theory was so appealing because if we knew the exact position and velocity of everything in the universe, we could predict the future.

For example, if we knew the position and velocity of the Moon, we could know its position in the future, and also its position in the past. This could be done by applying Newton's laws. Intuitively, Laplace imagined that if one knew every particle's state, then the future would be fully calculable.

This dream would soon be challenged. Some calculations made by Lord Rayleigh and Sir James Jeans suggested that a hot object must radiate energy at an infinite rate — something clearly wrong. In order to avoid this paradox, Max Planck proposed that energy could only be emitted in discrete packets, which he called quanta.

Each quantum had a certain amount of energy, which increased if the frequency of the wave was higher and decreased if it was lower.

This had severe implications for determinism when Werner Heisenberg developed his uncertainty principle. If one wanted to measure the position of a particle, one could use light. However, to determine position accurately, one would need shorter wavelengths, which imply higher energy photons.

Those photons would disturb the particle and change its velocity in a way that cannot be precisely predicted.

Heisenberg showed that the more precisely you try to measure a particle's position, the less accurately you can know its momentum, and vice versa.

This ended Laplace's dream of a fully deterministic universe and gave us a profound lesson:

> one certainly cannot predict future events exactly if one cannot even measure the present state of the universe precisely.
