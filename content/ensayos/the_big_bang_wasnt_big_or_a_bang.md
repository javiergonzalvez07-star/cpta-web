The Big Bang has been one of the most popular theories in science and has also been heavily used in films and popular culture.

However, the common image many people have of it is very wrong.

The development of this theory began when astronomers discovered something remarkable: galaxies are moving away from us, and also from each other.

This had already been predicted by Friedmann and later by Lemaître, who proposed that space itself is expanding.

According to Einstein's general relativity, mass curves spacetime. If matter is distributed across the universe, space itself can stretch, creating more distance between galaxies.

Galaxies are not really flying through empty space away from each other. Instead, the space between them is growing.

Once scientists knew the universe was expanding, a natural question appeared:

If we reverse time, what happens?

If the universe expands today, then in the past it must have been smaller and denser.

This idea successfully explains many observations:

- the abundance of light elements
- the cosmic microwave background
- large scale cosmic structure

But there is an important misunderstanding.

The Big Bang does **not** describe an explosion in empty space.

It describes the early expansion of space itself.

Nor does it necessarily imply a single point where all matter was concentrated.

If we go far enough back, our current theories stop working.

Before the first tiny fraction of a second, general relativity breaks down under extreme densities, and we would need a theory of quantum gravity — something physics still does not possess.

So in summary:

The Big Bang was not big, and it was not a bang.

It is our best model for how the universe expanded from an extremely hot, dense early state.
